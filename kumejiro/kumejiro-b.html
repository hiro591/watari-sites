The House

An Echizen-lacquer house, painting in gold for a thousand-year tradition

Kumejiro (Tsuchinao Shikki) works in Sabae, Fukui — heart of Echizen Shikki, a lacquerware tradition reaching back roughly fifteen centuries and named a government-designated traditional craft in 1975. Every bowl begins on a turned wood base, is brushed with natural urushi, then decorated by hand with maki-e: gold and coloured lacquer laid and polished, blossom by blossom.

Why it is special · the gold artistry

Maki-e — blossoms drawn in gold, then polished to a sheen

Maki-e is painting with metal. The artist draws each petal in lacquer, dusts pure gold and silver powder onto the wet line, lets it cure, then burnishes it back to a soft, deep lustre. The cherry blossoms here are individual brushwork — no two bowls in the set are identical.

  • Pure goldCherry blossoms painted and dusted in pure gold powder, with vermilion and coloured lacquer for the petals.
  • Polished maki-eThe raised gold is burnished by hand (togidashi-style finishing) for depth rather than flat brightness.
  • Natural urushiDeep black ground built from hand-brushed natural urushi over a turned wood base.
  • One of a kindEach bowl is hand-decorated; the set of five carries five individual hands of the same blossom.

Living with lacquer

Care & use

Natural urushi is durable and made to be used. A few habits keep the gold deep for generations.

01

Wash by hand

Warm water and a soft sponge; mild detergent is fine. No dishwasher, no abrasive pads.

02

Dry & gentle heat

Wipe dry with a soft cloth. Avoid microwaves, ovens and prolonged direct sun.

03

It improves with use

Regular use keeps the lustre alive; store loosely, not stacked hard against the maki-e. Specific guidance to confirm with the maker.
