The District

Kanaya-machi, where the furnaces never quite went cold

Takaoka has carried a tradition of cast-metal craft for around four hundred years. Kanaya-machi — the foundry quarter laid out in the Edo era — is where that tradition still keeps its address.

Walk the lattice-fronted machiya streets of Kanaya-machi and you are walking through a working memory of metal. This is the quarter the founders gave over to casting, and the craft it gathered has stayed close ever since — passed along workbenches, between hands, across generations of houses that learned to read fire and sand.

Ootera Kohachiro Shoten keeps its rooms here. The same building that holds the casting work opens, too, as a cafe and gallery, and hosts tin-casting workshops where visitors try the craft with their own hands — a quiet argument that heritage is something you keep doing, not something you display behind glass.

The figures and dates of the house’s own lineage are best confirmed with the maker; what is plain on the street is older than that — a district that has been answering to the word foundry for the better part of four centuries.

  • Roughly four centuries Cast metal as a living trade. Takaoka’s reputation for copperware is built and re-built, workshop by workshop.

The Craft

Cast in sand, finished by hand

Each Mini Eto figurine is sand-cast in bronze and then patinated by hand — which is why no two pieces wear quite the same surface.

  1. i.

    Pattern & mould

    A model of the animal is packed into casting sand, then drawn out to leave a clean negative ready to receive metal.

  2. ii.

    Pouring the bronze

    Molten bronze is poured into the sand mould and left to set into the small, solid form of the figure.

  3. iii.

    Filing & finishing

    Once cooled, the casting is broken out and worked back — seams filed, surfaces refined — until the animal reads clearly.

  4. iv.

    Patination by hand

    The surface is coloured by hand. Finishes are varied by design, so the same animal can arrive in different tones.

  5. v.

    The set, assembled

    Finished figures are gathered into their zodiac group and boxed — a small herd, no two identical.

  6. vi.

    Try it yourself

    In the machiya workshop, visitors cast their own tin pieces — the same logic of mould and metal, at a beginner’s scale.

Because each surface is patinated by hand, no two animals in the set are exactly alike.

The Living Zodiac

Mini Eto — the animals of the calendar, cast small

The zodiac is not a relic in Japan; it still names years, marks new-year gifts and turns up on cards and charms. Ootera’s Mini Eto sets carry that living tradition in bronze.

The twelve-animal set gathers the full cycle of the Japanese zodiac — one creature for each year, a small bronze herd that completes a familiar round.
