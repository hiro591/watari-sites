Why the clay matters

Unglazed high-iron Banko clay, left to do its work.

Banko-ware is known for its iron-rich clay. Fired unglazed, the porous surface is widely said to round off the astringency of sencha — a qualitative claim, but one the specialty-tea world has leaned on for generations. With regular use the pot seasons, settling into the teas you brew most.

  • 1The side-handle is the point. A kyusu’s side handle is the classic sencha-brewing form — poured with one hand, close to the cup.
  • 2No glaze, by design. The bare clay is what interacts with the tea; it is meant to age, not stay pristine.
  • 3Made where it has always been made. Thrown at Nankei Seitoen in Yokkaichi, Mie — 50+ years of kyusu.

How to brew

A short, modern sencha method.

A simple starting point for green tea in a kyusu — adjust to the leaf and to taste.

50°C 60°C 70–80°C · sencha off boil
01

Cool the water

Use water just off the boil, around 70–80°C, for sencha. Hotter water draws out more astringency.

02

Leaf & pour

Add leaf to the warmed pot, then pour the cooled water gently over it.

03

Steep short

Keep early steeps short — a brief rest is plenty. The side handle pours close and quick.

04

Pour to the last

Empty the pot fully each time, then re-steep. The leaf opens further with each round.
