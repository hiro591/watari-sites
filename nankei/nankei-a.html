The maker

Fifty years of one form, in Yokkaichi clay.

Nankei Seitoen is a Banko-ware pottery in Yokkaichi, Mie — a town whose name has been bound to the side-handle teapot for generations.

For more than fifty years the workshop has thrown kyusu and little else, refining a single object until each curve answers to the hand. The Anzu shape — apricot-round, low and settled — is theirs: thrown and finished by hand, then left unglazed so the clay itself remains the surface you hold.

Steep

Care & use

Living with unglazed clay.

A few quiet habits keep an unglazed pot at its best for decades.

None of this is fussy — it is simply the rhythm of a pot meant to be used every day.

  1. Rinse, don't scrub. Use warm water only — no soap and no detergent, which the porous clay can hold.
  2. Dry it open. Leave the lid off and let the pot air-dry fully before it is put away.
  3. Let it season. The clay will gradually take on the character of the tea you brew; this is intended, not a flaw.
  4. Keep it for sencha. Brewing one kind of tea lets the seasoning settle into a single, consistent note.
  5. Avoid the dishwasher and sudden temperature shocks. to confirm

What is the difference between the two strainers?

The stainless bottom-mesh is removable and very easy to clean; the integral fired-clay sasame is part of the pot itself, with a softer, more traditional pour. Either can be specified — let us know your preference when you ask.

Why is the pot left unglazed?

Unglazed high-iron Banko clay is traditionally valued for the way it is said to round the astringency of green tea, and it seasons gradually with use. These are qualitative, traditional observations rather than measured claims — we keep them measured on purpose.

How much tea does it actually make?

320 cc to the brim, or roughly 256 cc at a practical fill — about four small Japanese teacups in a single steep.
