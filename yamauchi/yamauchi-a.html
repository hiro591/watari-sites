The maker

A family vat in Mima

Aizome Yamauchi is a family natural-indigo workshop in Mima, Tokushima — a region long known for Awa indigo. They dye in a traditional sukumo fermentation vat: composted indigo leaves kept alive and warm, turned by hand. No synthetic indigo enters the work.

The workshop sits among the old udatsu merchant houses of the area, and is part of how Tokushima introduces its craft to visitors from abroad. Guests come to dip their own cloth; finished pieces sell out.

Yet the maker's own shop carries no English — no English story, no overseas ordering. This listing is prepared so that a piece from this vat can be understood, and asked about, from anywhere.

The living indigo

The blue begins as warmth, earth and patience

Before it is colour it is compost — fermented indigo leaves, kept alive in the vat and turned by hand. The depth you see in the cloth is built dip by dip from this living dye, oxidising to blue in the air each time it is lifted out.

Why this piece

Tied by hand, one of a kind

01 / Resist

Shibori, tied by hand

The pattern is made by binding the cloth before dyeing, so the indigo cannot reach the tied points. White-resist shapes emerge where the hand held the fabric closed.

02 / Dye

Natural Awa indigo

Colour from a sukumo fermentation vat rather than synthetic indigo — the depth and quiet variation that only a living dye gives.

03 / Cloth

Sheer and light

A silk-blend stole, sheer and lightweight, so the blue reads as colour and light rather than weight. Each piece is one of a kind.
