The Maker · Since 1908

A Tokyo cutting house, five generations at the wheel

Edo Kiriko Kobayashi has cut glass in Koto-ku, Tokyo since 1908. Edo Kiriko — the cut-glass tradition that took its name from old Edo — is a government-designated traditional craft of Japan, and every facet here is still cut by hand.

The technique is kabuse: clear crystal is sheathed in a thin skin of deep colour, then a craftsman opens that skin against a spinning wheel. Where the colour is cut away, light pours through; where it stays, the ruri holds its cobalt depth. It is patient, unforgiving work — a line, once cut, cannot be put back.

Why It Is Special

Cobalt over clear — read facet by facet

One glass carries two patterns and two readings of light: a chrysanthemum band that opens the crystal up bright, over a diamond-lattice body that holds the blue. Each is a separate sequence of cuts, made by eye and hand.

01 — The SkinKabuse overlay

A deep ruri layer is fused over clear crystal. Every facet you see is that colour deliberately removed to expose the crystal’s shine.

02 — The CrownKiku-tsunagi

A linked-chrysanthemum band rings the upper glass — fine, radiant cuts that throw the most light back toward the eye.

03 — The BodyYarai lattice

Below, an arrow / diamond-lattice grid keeps the cobalt deep and gives the glass its weight in the hand.

✦

One-of-a-kind, by definition

Because the lines are cut by hand against a wheel, no two glasses are identical. Small differences in the cut are the signature of a person, not a machine — yours is the only one exactly like it.

Care & Use

Treat it like the evening it’s for

◇

Pour

Whisky over a single large ice block, or chilled sake. The cut facets refract the colour as the level drops — the glass is part of the drink.

◇

Hand-wash in lukewarm water with a soft sponge; dry with a lint-free cloth. Avoid sudden temperature change and abrasive scrubbers. Dishwasher use: to confirm with the maker.

A Few Questions

Before you ask

Can I order or ask in English? +
Yes. English inquiries are handled for the maker by Watari — you write in English and receive replies in English. The studio itself has welcomed overseas interest; the finer details (shipping, payment, final figures) are confirmed with the maker for each request.
Is there an English / USD checkout? +
Not yet. Despite international press, there is no English-language or USD checkout and no standing international-shipping option. That is exactly why inquiries run through a person — so terms are set clearly, in English, before anything is agreed.
Is it genuine lead crystal? +
It is crystal glass. Whether it is genuine lead crystal is marked to confirm — we verify this with the maker rather than guess.
Why are some figures marked “to confirm”? +
Diameter, capacity, the lead-crystal question and the US-dollar estimate are not yet locked. We’d rather flag them honestly and finalise them with the maker than print a precise-looking number we can’t stand behind.
Will every glass look exactly like the photos? +
Closely, but not identically. Because each piece is hand-cut, small variations are inherent — that is the nature of the craft, not a flaw.
